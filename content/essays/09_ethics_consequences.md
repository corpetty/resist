## Introduction

[[Essay 3]] argued armed resistance usually fails strategically. [[Essay 8]] showed nonviolent movements succeed more often. But even when resistance can win—should it? At what cost?

[[Algerian Independence]] achieved freedom from France. Cost: 300,000-1.5 million dead, decades of authoritarian military rule, 1990s civil war killing 200,000 more. Was it worth it?

[[Indian Independence]] achieved freedom largely nonviolently. Cost: Partition killed hundreds of thousands, displaced millions. Gandhi was assassinated. Communal violence persists. Was it better than the alternative?

This essay confronts uncomfortable questions about costs, unintended consequences, moral responsibility, and whether there are limits to what resistance movements should pursue—even in pursuit of justice.

## The Consequentialist Question

Simplest ethical frame: did good outcomes outweigh costs?

**[[Tunisian Revolution]]:** Ben Ali fled, democracy established (fragile but functioning). Costs: 300+ killed during uprising, economic struggles persist, 2021 authoritarian backsliding. But compared to status quo (dictatorship), most Tunisians probably judged it worth it.

**[[Syrian Uprising]]:** Assad still in power. Costs: 500,000+ dead, half population displaced, cities destroyed, society traumatized. Even if Assad eventually falls, was this better than enduring dictatorship? Honestly—hard to argue it was.

From pure consequentialist view, Syria is catastrophic failure. The costs vastly exceed any plausible benefit.

But consequentialism has problems:

**Counterfactuals unknowable:** How do you know status quo would've been better? Maybe Assad would've killed half a million anyway maintaining power. Maybe prolonged dictatorship causes different traumas. Can't run controlled experiments.

**Who judges?** Syrian who lost family to barrel bombs has different assessment than one who fled to Europe. Victims vs. survivors vs. external observers have different stakes.

**Timeframe matters:** [[Chilean Resistance to Pinochet]] costs accumulated over 17 years. Worth it for democracy? Depends when you ask—victims tortured in 1970s vs. youth benefiting from post-1990 democracy have different perspectives.

**Unintended consequences:** [[Iranian Revolution]] overthrew brutal Shah. Created arguably worse Islamic Republic. Revolutionaries didn't intend authoritarian theocracy—but got it anyway. Are they responsible?

Consequentialism is essential consideration but insufficient alone.

## Deontological Limits: Some Things Are Just Wrong

Alternative frame: some acts are wrong regardless of consequences.

Torture is wrong—even if it might save lives. Targeting civilians is wrong—even in supposedly just wars. Using children as soldiers is wrong—even if your cause is righteous.

Many resistance movements violated these:

**[[Algerian Independence]]:** FLN terrorism targeted French civilians. Morally indefensible even if strategically understandable. French torture of Algerians also indefensible. Both sides committed atrocities.

**[[Syrian Uprising]]:** Opposition groups (not just ISIS) committed atrocities. Assad's barrel bombs and chemical weapons obviously monstrous. But opposition's crimes don't disappear because Assad worse.

**[[Irish Independence]]:** IRA bombings killed civilians. British repression also brutal. Competing atrocities don't justify each other.

From deontological view, means matter intrinsically. You can't torture your way to a just society. You can't massacre civilians for liberation. The means corrupt the ends.

But strict deontology has problems:

**Absolutism can be paralyzing:** If any violence is wrong, what do you do facing genocide? Jewish resistance to Holocaust used violence—was that wrong?

**Asymmetric application:** Holding resistance movements to standards states violate seems hypocritical. Why is state violence "legitimate" but resistance violence inherently wrong?

**Ignores structural violence:** Focusing on discrete acts (bombings, shootings) ignores slow violence of poverty, oppression, systemic racism that kill far more people. Is maintaining unjust status quo morally neutral?

Pure deontology is morally clear but strategically and practically problematic.

## Responsibility for Unintended Consequences

Harder question: Are movements responsible for unintended outcomes?

**[[Iranian Revolution]]:** Diverse coalition overthrew Shah. Khomeini and clergy hijacked revolution, established theocracy. Are secular leftists who participated responsible for Islamic Republic? They didn't want it, fought against it, many were killed by it.

**[[Egyptian Revolution 2011]]:** Protesters removed Mubarak. Military hijacked transition, staged coup, established Sisi dictatorship possibly worse than Mubarak. Are Tahrir Square activists responsible?

**[[Sudanese Revolution]]:** Removed Bashir. Military coup. Now civil war between rival military factions. Are resistance committees responsible for this catastrophe?

Arguments for responsibility:
- Movements should foresee risks
- Overthrowing regimes without capacity to shape transitions is reckless
- Creating power vacuums enables worst actors

Arguments against:
- Can't predict all consequences
- Responsibility lies with those who commit atrocities, not those who created conditions
- Moral hazard of requiring oppressed to ensure perfect outcomes before resisting

I lean toward: **partial responsibility for foreseeable outcomes, but primary responsibility lies with actors who commit atrocities.**

[[Egyptian Revolution 2011]] activists couldn't have prevented military coup entirely—but could have been more strategic about securing democratic institutions. They bear some responsibility for insufficient planning, but Sisi bears primary responsibility for the coup.

## The Problem of Incrementalism vs. Revolution

Some argue: better to pursue gradual reform than revolutionary change with unpredictable consequences.

**[[US Civil Rights Movement]]:** Worked within system (courts, legislation, federal intervention). Achieved major changes without civil war. Incremental approach successful.

But:
- Only worked because [[Essay 6]]'s mechanisms were available (economic leverage, sympathetic federal government, moral pressure working on some whites)
- Even this "nonviolent" movement faced brutal repression
- Changes were incomplete (economic inequality persists, mass incarceration, police violence continues)
- Required extraordinary patience from people suffering

Could [[Syrian Uprising]] have pursued incremental reform? Assad crushed even modest dissent. Incrementalism requires regime willing to reform—not always available.

Could [[Hong Kong Protests]] have accepted gradual erosion? Beijing was systematically dismantling autonomy. Incrementalism means slow loss rather than resistance.

Incrementalism vs. revolution is false binary—depends on whether incremental path exists. Some contexts offer no gradual options.

## Limits of Sacrifice: How Much Should Be Asked?

[[Essay 3]] noted [[Gandhi]] said he'd prefer Indians endure British rule than resist violently if violence was only option. Easy for him to say—he wasn't suffering worst brutality, and he had nonviolent option available.

What's reasonable to ask of people suffering oppression?

**Heroic sacrifice:** the Montgomery Bus Boycott participants walked miles to work for year rather than ride segregated buses. Enormous sacrifice—but time-limited, clear goal, collective sharing of burden.

**Sustained sacrifice:** [[Polish Solidarity]] members risked careers, imprisonment, families' welfare for nine years. Some imprisoned, some killed. Fair to ask this?

**Ultimate sacrifice:** [[Syrian Uprising]] peaceful protesters faced live ammunition, chemical weapons, torture. Should they have continued nonviolent resistance unto death to maintain moral high ground? Or was armed resistance justified even if strategically counterproductive?

I don't have clean answer. But principles:

**1. Sacrifice should be voluntary:** Movements shouldn't coerce participation. [[Gandhi]]'s fasts were his choice—he didn't force others to fast unto death.

**2. Risks should be shared:** Leaders demanding sacrifice while staying safe is morally suspect. [[US Civil Rights Movement]] leaders faced same dangers as participants.

**3. Limits exist:** Asking people to endure genocide nonviolently is obscene. There are limits to moral obligation.

**4. Strategic question matters:** Sacrifice for likely victory different from sacrifice for symbolic gesture. If armed resistance in Syria made things worse—was it justified? Intentions don't erase consequences.

## Transition Justice and Reconciliation

When resistance succeeds, what ethical obligations exist toward former opponents?

**Retribution:** French Resistance executed collaborators post-liberation. [[Algerian Independence]] FLN purged "collaborators." Natural impulse—but risks cycles of violence.

**Accountability:** [[South African Anti-Apartheid]] Truth and Reconciliation Commission attempted accountability without retribution. Imperfect—many victims felt justice denied. But prevented revenge killings.

**Amnesty:** [[Chilean Resistance to Pinochet]] negotiated transition included amnesty for military. Morally troubling—torturers escaped justice. But enabled peaceful transition.

**Lustration:** [[Velvet Revolution]] and [[East German Revolution]] banned communist officials from positions. Less severe than prosecution, more than amnesty. Middle path.

No perfect answer. Tradeoffs between:
- Justice for victims
- Prevention of future atrocities (accountability as deterrent)
- Stability and reconciliation
- Elite incentives to negotiate transitions

[[South African Anti-Apartheid]] chose reconciliation over retribution. Prevented civil war, but left economic injustice intact. Was that right choice? Victims of apartheid have legitimate grievances that TRC didn't fully address.

## When Not to Resist

Hardest question: Are there contexts where resistance causes more harm than endurance?

**[[Syrian Uprising]]:** If activists in 2011 could foresee half a million dead, would peaceful endurance of Assad have been preferable? Gut reaction: no, dictatorship is unacceptable. But half a million corpses is obscene cost.

**Honest assessment:** For many Syrians, the uprising made things catastrophically worse than status quo. This is tragedy—not because resistance was wrong, but because all options were terrible.

**[[Myanmar 2021]]:** Ongoing. If resistance fails and civil war continues for years killing tens of thousands—will Myanmar be better off? Unknowable now, but future historians might conclude resistance, while morally understandable, was catastrophically costly.

I resist claiming oppressed people should accept oppression. But honesty requires admitting: **sometimes resistance causes more suffering than it alleviates.**

This doesn't mean:
- Oppressed are responsible for oppressor's violence
- Resistance is wrong
- People should accept injustice

It means:
- Consequences matter morally
- Strategic thinking about costs is ethical obligation
- Sometimes tragedies have no good options

## Conclusion

Resistance movements pursue justice but operate in messy reality where:
- Good intentions don't guarantee good outcomes
- Victory can produce worse oppression
- Sacrifice can be wasted on failed causes
- Unintended consequences can be catastrophic

Ethical resistance requires:
- Honesty about costs and risks
- Responsibility for foreseeable consequences
- Limits on means (some tactics wrong regardless)
- Strategic thinking about whether resistance improves situation
- Humility about ability to predict outcomes

[[Syrian Uprising]] pursued justice and agency. I can't say they were wrong—I don't live under Assad. But the cost has been staggering, and it's not clear Syria is better off.

[[Tunisian Revolution]] achieved more with less cost. Strategy, context, and luck all mattered.

Ethics and strategy intersect: choosing tactics with best chance of success while respecting moral limits.
